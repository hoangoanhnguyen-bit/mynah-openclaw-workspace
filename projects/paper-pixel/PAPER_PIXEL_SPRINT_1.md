## Sprint Hypothesis

The strongest first message is not "I made an AI playbook."

It is:

> I am a non-technical mom who built games with my 6-year-old, and it changed how I think about screen time.

This should resonate with parent/education audiences because it addresses a real tension:
- Parents know AI matters.
- Parents worry about screen time.
- Parents do not want another passive app.
- Parents want concrete, low-friction ways to help their kids build future skills.

Subject options:

Body:

Hi [Name],

I wanted to share a small project that might fit your audience.

I am a non-technical mom, and I recently started using AI tools like Gemini and Claude to build simple web games with my 6-year-old.

The turning point was when he came to me with a spelling game idea. I said, "Ok, mommy will build it for you."

He said: "No. I want to build the app with you."

That changed the whole exercise.

He started sketching game ideas on paper. He learned that if he wanted the AI to build something, he had to describe it clearly. He played the first version, noticed what was wrong, and told the AI how to fix it.

One of the games, Letterbug, was approved for classroom use by his school principal. Another game, AquaPath, started as a crayon sketch. A third, Fruit Boomerang, he mostly spoke into existence with a microphone. At one point he told Gemini, "there's no fruit in level 1" - basically his first bug report - and the AI fixed it.

I turned the whole process into a short playbook for other parents: Teach Your Kid to Think With AI.

The point is not to turn kids into software engineers. It is to help them practice clear communication, critical thinking, iteration, and creative confidence using AI as a tool.

If you think this would be useful for your audience, I would be happy to send you a review copy or write a short guest post about what we learned.

Best,
Oanh

## Social Post Drafts

### LinkedIn / Founder Post

My 6-year-old filed his first bug report this year.

Not in Jira. To Gemini.

We were building a simple game together using AI. He had described a game with bouncing fruit and boomerangs, mostly by speaking into the microphone because he cannot type fluently yet.

The first version had a bug.

He looked at the screen and said: "there's no fruit in level 1."

Gemini fixed it in 34 seconds.

That moment stuck with me.

Not because the game was technically impressive. It was not. It was a small web game.

But my son had done something important:
- noticed what was wrong
- described the problem clearly
- gave feedback to a tool
- tested the result

That is the same feedback loop adults use at work every day.

I do not think every child needs to become a software engineer. But I do think every child will need to know how to direct AI, judge its output, and communicate clearly.

So I wrote down the exact process we used to build four games together, including the prompts, mistakes, sketches, and iteration steps.

It became a playbook for non-technical parents who want to turn screen time into creative time.

https://playbook.paperandpixel.studio/
